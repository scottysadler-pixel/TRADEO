Explain the Chameleon

Idea: different market conditions need different rules.

  • Low volatility (calm): follow the trend. Price above 20-day moving average → LONG; below → SHORT.
  • High volatility (jumpy): fade extremes. Price >1.5% below MA → buy dip; >1.5% above → sell rally.
  • Medium volatility: no edge, stay FLAT.

Why it can be useful: beginners tend to pick one style and apply it everywhere. This is a simple way to adapt.

Why it can fail: regime detection is lagging; it uses 20-day history so it's slow to react to sudden shifts.

Explain the Catchup

Idea: Australia exports a lot of commodities, so AUD/USD tends to follow gold. When gold moves sharply and AUD hasn't, the theory says AUD is about to catch up.

  • Gold up >2% in 5 days and AUD up <1% → LONG AUD (expecting catch-up).
  • Gold down >2% in 5 days and AUD down <1% → SHORT AUD.
  • Otherwise → FLAT.

Why it can fail: the gold-AUD link isn't constant. US dollar strength, Chinese demand, and rate differentials can break it. The 90-day correlation number shows how tight the link is right now.

Explain rate differential

When the RBA's rate is higher than the Fed's, holding AUD pays more interest than holding USD — a small tailwind for AUD/USD (the “carry”). When Fed rates are higher, the carry favours USD.

Why agreement matters

The two strategies look at different things: Chameleon reads AUD/USD price action, Catchup reads gold vs AUD. When they agree, you have two independent reasons for the same trade. When they disagree, it usually means conditions are unclear and FLAT is the honest answer.

What is “vol percentile”?

We rank today's 20-day volatility against the last ~year. 90th percentile means only 10% of days in the last year were more volatile — a genuinely jumpy market. Below 25th = calm. The Chameleon flips its rules based on this.

What is this feed and how reliable is it?

GDELT is a project that indexes global news. It's free and genuinely near-real-time (minutes), but quality varies: you'll see everything from major outlets to small blogs. Use it to get a sense of what stories are circulating, not as a single source of truth.

The search is wide: we include AUD, RBA, Fed, US inflation, gold, and risk-off keywords, because any of those can move AUD/USD.

What this engine actually does
  • For each day, it asks: what signal does the strategy give?
  • When the signal flips (FLAT → LONG etc.), it enters at the next day's close.
  • Stop and take-profit are checked against the daily close only (crude — real markets have intraday spikes).
  • Position size is chosen so a full stop-loss = your risk-% of account.
  • No broker fees, no overnight swap, no spread. Real trades lose 2-5 pips to the spread alone.

What does this app actually do?

It reads free public data (AUD/USD daily rate from the European Central Bank, gold daily close from stooq, recent news headlines from GDELT, hand-maintained RBA/Fed target rates), runs two simple rule-based strategies on that data, and shows you a daily LONG / SHORT / FLAT view for AUD/USD.

The Sandbox lets you replay the same data as if you'd followed the rules, to see would this have worked in the last 30-260 days. The Journal lets you log your own (demo or real) trades. Everything is local to your browser except the news and price fetches.

Is the data live?

Short answer: daily, not tick.

  • AUD/USD (Frankfurter / ECB) is published once per business day, ~16:00 Central European Time. During Australian trading hours today's number usually hasn't been published yet — you'll see yesterday's close.
  • Gold (stooq) is the previous session's close.
  • News (GDELT) is near-real-time (minutes).

For learning and demo trading this is fine. For live trading you'd need a paid/intraday API — that would be a separate feature.

Is it safe to trade with real money based on this?

No. This is a learning and observation tool. The strategies are simple rules, not an edge anyone should bet serious money on. Use demo accounts, expect to lose often at first, and treat the numbers here as prompts to think, not instructions.

Glossary

AUD/USD

Number of US dollars 1 Australian dollar buys. 0.6800 means 1 AUD = 68 US cents.

Pip

Smallest commonly quoted move. For AUD/USD a pip is 0.0001 (0.6800 → 0.6801).

LONG / SHORT / FLAT

LONG = buy AUD (profit if AUD goes up). SHORT = sell AUD (profit if AUD goes down). FLAT = stay out.

Stop loss

Price at which your broker automatically closes a losing trade. Position size is chosen so hitting the stop = your risk-% of account. Non-negotiable if you want to survive.

Take profit

Price at which your broker automatically closes a winning trade. Set as a multiple of stop (1.5× is a common starting point — you need to win more often than you lose to be profitable at 1×).

Moving average (MA)

Average price over the last N days. 20-day MA smooths out noise and shows direction. Price > MA generally = uptrend.

Volatility

How much prices wiggle. Here: standard deviation of the last 20 daily returns. Higher = wider daily ranges.

Regime

A label (low / med / high) for how volatile the recent market has been. Different regimes reward different strategies.

Correlation

How closely two things move together. +1 = lockstep, 0 = unrelated, -1 = opposite. Gold and AUD are typically +0.3 to +0.6.

Carry / rate differential

Higher-yielding currency pays interest to its holder. If RBA > Fed, holding AUD earns more than holding USD, so AUD tends to have a small tailwind. Breaks down fast in risk-off markets.

Backtest

Replaying historical data to see how a strategy would have performed. Should be a starting point for real trading, never an end point — the future rarely matches the past.

Demo account

Broker account with fake money. Same platform, same prices, no risk. Use this for at least a month before any real capital.

What can go wrong

Strategy stops working

Simple rules work until they don't. A new macro regime (like a rate-cutting cycle, or a China shock) can make a previously profitable rule lose money for months.

Slippage and spread

Your broker doesn't fill you at the mid-price. Spread + slippage might cost you 3-5 pips per trade. Our Sandbox ignores this, so real results are always worse.

Overfitting backtests

If you tune the parameters until the backtest looks great, you've probably just memorised history. The honest test is running the strategy forward in real time (on demo) for weeks/months.

Leverage

FX is usually traded on margin. Same rule: if 50:1 leverage lets you feel thrills, it will also blow up your account. Start with micro lots and almost no leverage.
